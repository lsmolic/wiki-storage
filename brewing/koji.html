Koji

Koji ( 麹,. chinese - qú ) is the name for any substrate that has had the Aspergillus Oryzae mold grown on it. This mold has been cultivated for thousands of years and produces some amazing tastes that have defined Asian cooking, and when we specifically talk about Japan, it is responsible for what is often described as the ‘umami’ flavor.

In sake brewing, koji-kin (麹菌), or tane-koji (種麹)as it is also referred, is a spore that is spread out over freshly steamed rice. Over 40-55 hours, using heat and temperature control, the koji-kin will grow into a small fungus that spreads over the surface of the grains. When a soft-looking fuzz appears over all the grains, we refer to this moldy rice as koji. 

Koji-kin, the reproductive spore we use to inoculate, is produced by mature Koji when it is in its final stages of growth and to date, only about 7-10 companies in the world produce Koji-kin for purchase, as quality control and strain selection is an extremely difficult art.

If you are curious to learn more, the Soyinfo Center released a comprehensive study about the history of koji. There is also a growing wiki mentioned a few times on this site, which contains a koji glossary, among other resources that can help further your understanding on this topic.

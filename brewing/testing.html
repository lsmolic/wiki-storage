Sando (total acidity)

Testing the acidity of sake by titration

 

Amino-sando (total amino acid) by Sorensen's Formalin Titration

Invented in 1907 by S.P.L. Sorensen. It is a technique of titrating amino acids with sodium hydroxide in the presence of CH2O (formaldehyde). It helps in finding protein present in samples.

Amino acid reacts with formaldehyde to form methelene amino acid and water. In this reaction, amino group (-NH3) interacts with CH2O, resulting in R-N=CH2 (methelene amino group). The remaining carboxylic group (-COOH) is then titrated with a base (sodium hydroxide) and the volume of base used corresponds to the amount of protein content in the sample. The equations below show the reactions between formaldehyde and amino acid, and methelene amino acid and sodium hydroxide.

H3N-R-COOH + CH2O  →  H2C=N-R-COOH + H2O

H2C=N-R-COOH + NaOH → H2C=N-R-COO-Na+ + H2O
